What Makes a Novel a Classic?

An exploration using the Google Ngram dataset to find out what makes classic novels different from the rest

What makes a book a “classic”? How should we define what works belong in the Western literary canon? From scholars to AP lit students, these questions have been debated in both academic and public circles for centuries. To some, the classics represent the pinnacle of human literary expression, works that deserve to be taught because they hold some inherent objective value. Others reject the notion of the “classics,” arguing that it is an outdated term to use in the discussion of literature.

In any case, even if we cannot agree on what defines a “classic,” or if this is even a term we should be using anymore, it is hard to deny that there are certain books that have had a clear, and, as we will soon see, a measurable impact on Western culture. Maybe a better question to start with, then, is this: how did these works become so ingrained in Western culture? Or, what makes a “classic” different from other novels in terms of their cultural impact?

To try to answer these questions, we compiled a list of 119 widely agreed upon Western classics, and, using the Google Ngram dataset, collected data on how mentions of their titles in other written works has changed over time.
